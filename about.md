# Pile

Pile is a semi-realistic nuclear reactor simulator that runs in your browser. Three reactor types share a common physics engine: a 1D axial point-kinetics model with six delayed-neutron precursor groups per axial node, fuel/clad/coolant thermal feedback with type-specific coefficients, iodine-135 to xenon-135 dynamics on the real 6.7-hour and 9.2-hour timescales, burnup-dependent kinetics and excess reactivity, ANS-5.1 decay heat, IAPWS-IF97 saturation temperatures, and coupled plant systems from core heat source through heat sink and grid demand. The model is intentionally lumped rather than CFD- or RELAP-grade, but the important state variables are coupled rather than scripted.

The three reactor types are a Westinghouse-class pressurized water reactor at 3411 MWth, a graphite-moderated RBMK-1000 at 3200 MWth with boiling water in pressure tubes and a direct turbine cycle, and a molten salt reactor modeled at MSRE scale with fuel dissolved in FLiBe. The default state is hot full-power equilibrium: delayed precursors, iodine, xenon, decay heat, burnup, and RBMK operating void are populated and then snapshotted as reactivity references so the initial reactor is critical by construction without pretending those inventories are absent. The PWR is strongly negative-feedback and has a four-loop primary system. The RBMK has graphite-temperature feedback, a low-power positive void coefficient, steam quality separated from void fraction, and graphite-tipped rods whose lower section can add positive reactivity during insertion. The MSR has very strong fuel-temperature feedback, circulating delayed-neutron precursors that decay partly outside the core, off-gas xenon removal, an intermediate salt loop, and a freeze plug that can drain fuel salt into a passively cooled drain tank.

The PWR plant model is the most developed. It includes CVCS charging/letdown and boration, reactor-coolant-pump seal failure, ECCS with HHSI/LHSI/accumulators/RHR, RWST and containment-sump suction, a dynamic pressurizer with PORV/safety-valves/PRT, four steam generators with three-element feedwater control, AFW trains, main feedwater and condensate pumps, regenerative feedwater heaters, SG tube plugging/rupture, MSIVs/ADVs/condenser bypass, staged turbine-generator/load rejection, EDGs, batteries, switchyard undervoltage relays, CCW/SW, large-dry containment sprays and fan coolers, and spent-fuel-pool cooling. RBMK and MSR currently keep simpler balance-of-plant branches while retaining the core physics that distinguishes them.

The reactor protection system runs scram and warning channels in parallel: high local flux, short positive period, low DNBR for water-cooled cores, high coolant outlet temperature, pressurizer pressure and level, steam-generator level, containment pressure, actual reactor coolant flow, turbine overspeed, seal LOCA, manual scram, and loss of off-site power, plus warning-only channels such as ORM, flow excursion, SI/RWST/NPSH, CVCS, EDG, battery, grid/vital-AC, CCW/SW, AFW, MFW, SGTR, secondary valves, containment spray/temperature, and spent-fuel-pool state. The annunciator panel latches channels until reset. The reactivity stack decomposes rods, boron, Doppler, moderator, void, xenon, and burnup effects as signed live contributions, so power maneuvers visibly turn into xenon and temperature-coefficient problems rather than magic numbers.

## Faults and procedures

The plant is presented as an interactive schematic: clicking any component opens an inspector with that component's readouts, controls, and — where applicable — fault injection. The current operator-training layer is deliberately smaller than the future scenario engine. PWR balance-of-plant failures that already have physics behind them include a stuck-open PORV, closed AFW discharge MOVs, loss of component/service water, loss of spent-fuel-pool cooling, EDG failure, and main-feedwater trip; direct plant actions include per-loop RCP trips, loop isolation, SGTR injection, feedwater-heater isolation, load rejection, and turbine-governor fault. Each lives in the inspector of the component it belongs to. These are command-level injections rather than scripted replays: you choose the failure, watch the plant respond, then use the procedure action bundles to stabilize it.

The procedure action bundles live in the reactor-core inspector and collect the immediate actions of a response procedure into one button. E-0 scrams the reactor and forces AFW/SI. E-1 closes the PORV block valve, fires SI, and disables pressurizer heaters. E-1.3 aligns sump recirculation and RHR. The SBO action starts the EDGs, sheds vital loads, starts AFW, and verifies the turbine-driven AFW block valve open. Spent-fuel-pool cooling restoration and diverse makeup are operated from the spent-fuel-pool inspector. A full scenario library with timelines, objectives, watch/play replays, and start-state presets is deferred to Wave 3.

## Accessibility

The toolbar, the left-rail controls, and every component inspector are keyboard-operable; sliders accept arrow keys and Home/End, and buttons are reachable in tab order. The scram action is bound to Shift+S regardless of focus, and the time-acceleration control has standard keyboard navigation. Selecting a component on the plant schematic currently uses a pointer (wheel to zoom, drag to pan); keyboard selection of schematic components is a known gap. Color-coded displays -- fuel temperature, coolant temperature, reactivity sign -- are also disambiguated by position and numeric value. The high-contrast theme toggle is in the toolbar. ARIA labels are applied to the major interactive controls. Known hazards: the plant schematic, trend plots, and readouts animate while the simulator is running, and newly latched trips blink briefly before going solid. Use pause or single-step to stop motion.
